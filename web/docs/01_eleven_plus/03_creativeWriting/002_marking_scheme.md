# What Are 11+ Creative Writing Examiners Really Looking For?

Creative writing is, by nature, subjective. So how do examiners mark it in an objective way? How can something as personal and imaginative as storytelling be measured with scores and checklists?

It’s a challenge that often leaves both parents and children asking:
- “What exactly are they looking for?”
- “What can make my story stand out over someone else’s?”

While creative writing may feel personal and open-ended, examiners use clear criteria to make their marking fair and consistent.

In this guide, we’ll explain how creative writing is assessed in the 11+ exam and how you can help your child prepare with confidence using practical strategies.

---

## 1. Structure Matters

One of the biggest mistakes children make is treating creative writing as an opportunity to let their imaginations lead, with no plan.

Examiners reward writing that’s **structured, purposeful and has a clear and simple plot**.

### They want to see:
- A clear structure with a beginning, a middle and an end  
- A sense of purpose: why are we reading this story or description? What’s the mood or the key event?  
- Paragraphs that make sense  

> Each paragraph should have one clear job (e.g. moving the story along or developing the scene)

Creative ideas are great, but if the writing goes off on a tangent or doesn’t have a clear ending, it won’t score well.

⚠️ Especially avoid the dreaded ending:  
> “...and then I woke up.”

Planning, plot and structure are what turn imagination into high-scoring writing.

---

## 2. Vocabulary (but not too much!)

Rich vocabulary helps writing stand out—but examiners are **not** counting how many “big words” are used.

They are looking for words that:
- Work well  
- Are used correctly  
- Fit the context  

### They want to see:
- Descriptive language that paints a clear picture  
- More than just adjectives → powerful verbs and nouns  
- Quality over quantity  

> Choosing the *right* word matters more than choosing the *fanciest* word.

---

## 3. Sentence Variety Is Key

Using a mix of sentence types makes writing more engaging and easier to read.

If all sentences sound the same, the writing feels flat and repetitive.

Sentence variety shows:
- Control of language  
- Reader awareness  
- Higher writing skill  

### They want to see:
- Short, punchy sentences for drama  
- Medium and longer sentences for description  
- Different sentence openings  
- Occasional rhetorical questions or exclamations (when appropriate)  

---

## 4. Does It Feel “Finished”?

A well-planned, satisfying ending often scores higher.

Many children:
- Run out of time  
- Run out of ideas  
- Or simply stop  

This makes the piece feel rushed or incomplete.

A strong ending shows:
- Planning  
- Control  
- Clear direction  

### Good endings should include:
- Reflection on what has happened  
- A link back to something earlier in the piece  
- A clear emotional impact  

> The reader should feel something:  
funny, tense, exciting or emotional  

Even a single thoughtful final sentence can make the piece feel complete.

---

## 5. SPAG: Getting the Basics Right

Spelling, Punctuation and Grammar (**SPAG**) are still hugely important.

Many schools allocate specific marks just for technical accuracy.

### They want to see:
- Correct punctuation (commas, apostrophes, speech marks)  
- Accurate spelling of common tricky words  
- Evidence of checking and editing  

> Reading work back to spot mistakes is a key habit.

---

## 6. Originality Isn’t About Crazy Plots

Parents often ask:
> “Does it have to be an original idea to get top marks?”

The truth:
👉 It’s not about the idea—it’s about how it’s written.

Examiners only see one piece of writing, so:
- It’s okay to reuse or adapt ideas  
- Familiar plots are fine if written well  

A clear, well-written story will always beat:
> A confusing story with poor punctuation

### Examiners look for originality in:
- The narrator’s voice  
- A fresh perspective on everyday situations  
- Vivid, clear descriptions  

---

## 🔥 Final Takeaway

Creative writing is not marked on imagination alone.

Examiners are looking for:
- Structure  
- Clarity  
- Control  
- Accuracy  
- A sense of completion  

👉 A simple, well-written story will always outperform a complicated, messy one.

# Typical 11+ Creative Writing Mark Schemes

In this example, children are marked out of **30**.

- Up to **20 marks** → Content and Style  
- Up to **10 marks** → Spelling and Grammar  

👉 Children are rewarded for the **quality**, not the quantity, of their writing.

---

## 📝 Content and Style (out of 20)

### ⭐ 20–19 marks:
- Focus on the task is excellent  
- Writing is enjoyable with flair  
- Tone, mood and atmosphere are successfully controlled  
- Vocabulary is consistently impressive  
- Figurative and sensory language is outstanding  
- Characterisation is convincing and sustained  

---

### ⭐ 18–16 marks:
- Focus on the task is good  
- Writing is strong  
- Tone, mood and atmosphere are mostly controlled  
- Vocabulary is carefully considered  
- Figurative and sensory language is sustained  
- Characterisation is clear  

---

### ⭐ 15–12 marks:
- Focus on the task is competent  
- Tone, mood and atmosphere are generally varied and convincing  
- Vocabulary is competent  
- Figurative and sensory language are attempted with some success  
- Characterisation has been attempted, but might be unconvincing  
- (At the lower end) may be contradictory  

---

### ⭐ 11–8 marks:
- Focus on the task is variable  
- Writing is straightforward  
- Tone, mood and atmosphere have been attempted  
- Vocabulary is straightforward  
- Figurative and sensory language is rarely used  
- Characterisation is straightforward  

---

### ⭐ 7–3 marks:
- Focus on the task is limited  
- Tone, mood and atmosphere are limited and uncontrolled  
- Vocabulary is basic or repetitive  
- There is no effective imagery or sensory language  
- Characterisation is limited  

---

### ⭐ 2–1 marks:
- There is little content that can be rewarded  

---

## ✍️ Spelling and Grammar (out of 10)

### ⭐ 10–9 marks:
- Punctuation, spelling and grammar are highly secure  
- Vocabulary choice is ambitious and accurate  
- Sentence structures are varied successfully  
- Paragraphs are used for effect  

---

### ⭐ 8–7 marks:
- Punctuation, spelling and grammar are mostly secure  
- Vocabulary choice is expressive and mostly accurate  
- Sentence structures are varied mostly with success  
- Paragraphs are sometimes used for effect  

---

### ⭐ 6–4 marks:
- Punctuation, spelling and grammar errors do not affect understanding  
- Vocabulary choice is competent, but less expressive  
- Some attempt to vary sentence structures  
- Paragraphs are used, but not for effect  

---

### ⭐ 3–2 marks:
- Errors may affect understanding  
- Vocabulary choice is unimaginative  
- Sentence structures are unvaried or incorrectly punctuated  
- Some attempt to use paragraphs  

---

### ⭐ 1 mark:
- Punctuation, spelling and grammar are significantly inaccurate  
- Vocabulary choice is limited  
- Sentence structures affect understanding  
- Little or no attempt to use paragraphs
