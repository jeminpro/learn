# 11 Plus Writing Genres and Why Audience Matters – A Quick Guide

---

## Introduction

The 11 Plus writing slot can feel daunting. However, many tasks repeat familiar patterns. Children who quickly identify the genre, adjust their tone and layout and adhere to a tight plan may score more reliably than those who write in a uniform style.

At home, try mixing your practice between handwriting and typing. Typing has many advantages when working at home, especially when you’re learning how to edit. As you approach the tests, it may help to handwrite.

---

## ✍️ 11 Plus Story Tasks – Plan with ‘CAPRE’ and Keep It Simple

Stories are often the most difficult to write under pressure. Children freeze. The blank page is daunting.

Understanding:
- **Story archetypes** (familiar characters, symbols or situations across cultures)  
- **Tropes** (common story ideas readers expect)  

…can help generate ideas.

### Key Approach:
- Limit to **1–2 characters**
- Use **one point of view**
- Keep the **plot simple**
- Introduce character and goal quickly  

### Use CAPRE:
- **Character** – one clear viewpoint  
- **Action** – start in motion  
- **Problem** – a snag or change  
- **Resolution** – how the character responds  
- **Ending** – satisfying closing moment  

### “Show and Tell”
We prefer **Show *and* Tell**:
- Too much showing → unclear  
- Too much telling → dull  
- Balance is key  

### Suggested Timing:
- 3 minutes → plan  
- 20 minutes → write  
- 5 minutes → check  

👉 Aim for **one clean plot**, not multiple ideas.

## 📝 11 Plus Letter Writing – Tone and Layout That Fit the Audience

### Identify the audience first:

#### Formal Letters:
- Structured layout (address, date, greeting, purpose, close)  
- Measured, precise diction  
- Avoid contractions  

#### Informal Letters:
- Conversational tone  
- Short paragraphs  
- Personal but relevant  

### Practice Tip:
Write the **same topic twice**:
- Once to a teacher or MP  
- Once to a friend  

👉 Compare tone, vocabulary and structure  

---

## 📓 11 Plus Diary Entries – Voice, Feeling, Shape

- Usually written in **first person**  
- Start with a **date**  
- Use a **consistent, believable voice**  

### Structure:
- Set-up  
- Key moment  
- Reflection  

👉 If writing as a character → match their voice, not yours  

---

## 🖼️ 11 Plus Description – Showing as the Default

Focus on:
- Atmosphere  
- Detail  
- Vocabulary in context  

Even without a plot, ensure:
- Clear focus  
- Sense of progression  

### Tips:
- Use **sensory details**  
- Vary sentence length  
- Avoid overloading adjectives  

### Practice Exercise:
Describe the same place:
- Calm vs tense  
- Happy vs sad  

👉 Notice how language changes  

---

## 🎭 11 Plus Applied Comprehension – Writing in Role

Write from a character’s viewpoint after reading a passage.

### Before writing:
Note:
- Age  
- Situation  
- Emotional state  
- Goal  

### Key Rule:
- Keep voice consistent with character  

### Practice:
- Start with familiar texts  
- Then move to unseen extracts  

---

## ⚖️ 11 Plus Discursive Essays – Balanced, Reasoned, Clear

### Structure:
- Introduction  
- Viewpoint one  
- Viewpoint two  
- Conclusion  

### Use discourse markers:
- “To begin with…”  
- “On the one hand…”  
- “However…”  
- “In contrast…”  
- “Nevertheless…”  

### Tip:
Avoid unsupported generalisations  

---

## 📢 11 Plus Persuasive Writing – Take a Stand and Justify It

### Key Difference:
- **Discursive** → balanced  
- **Persuasive** → one strong side  

### Structure (PREP):
- **Point** – clear opinion  
- **Reason** – why it matters  
- **Evidence** – example or scenario  
- **Point again** – stronger restatement  

### Tips:
- Match tone to audience  
- Use confident but respectful language  
- Include counterargument (if time allows)  

### Avoid:
- Exaggeration  
- Name-calling  

---

## 🧾 Less Common 11 Plus Tasks Worth Practising

### Instructions:
- Use imperative verbs  
- Clear sequence  
- Write for a beginner  

### News Report:
- Headline  
- Who, what, where, when, why, how  
- Neutral tone  
- Short paragraphs + quotes  

### Speech / Review:
- Speech → spoken style, call to action  
- Review → summary + evaluation + recommendation  

### School Magazine:
- Semi-formal  
- Clear headings  
- Short paragraphs  

### Playscript:
- Speaker labels  
- Minimal narration  
- Stage directions in brackets  

### Recount:
- Chronological order  
- Time connectives  
- Consistent tense/person  

---

## 🧠 11 Plus Exam Writing – Habits That Protect Marks

- Follow the brief exactly  
- Underline key words  
- Plan (3 min), write (~20 min), check (5 min)  
- Aim ~350–400 words (varies by school)  
- Use vocabulary accurately  
- Check punctuation and tense  
- Maintain neat presentation

# Who Are You Writing For? Why Audience Changes Everything

---

## What is Audience?

Audience = the person reading the writing.

A common issue:
Children write to complete the task, not communicate with a reader.

👉 When audience is clear:
- Tone improves  
- Vocabulary improves  
- Structure improves  

---

## 📓 Diary Entries and Audience

- Audience = writer  
- Personal, private tone  
- No over-explaining  

### If writing as a character:
- Match their voice  
- Think about their feelings and perspective  

---

## ✍️ Story Writing and Audience

Stories aim to:
👉 **Create an emotional effect**

Children must:
- Read the prompt carefully  
- Match tone and theme  
- Keep structure clear  

### Important distinction:
- **Story** → problem/change  
- **Recount** → sequence of events  

---

## 📝 Letter Writing and Audience

### To a friend:
- Relaxed  
- Personal  
- Conversational  

### To a headteacher:
- Formal  
- Respectful  
- Structured  

👉 Same topic, different tone  

---

## 📢 Persuasive Writing and Audience

Arguments change depending on audience.

Example:
- To sibling → casual  
- To teacher → formal + structured  

### Strong writing includes:
- Clear paragraphs  
- Logical flow  
- Discourse markers  

---

## 🧾 Information Writing and Audience

Common mistake:
👉 Listing facts without thinking about reader  

### Adjust based on audience:
- Younger reader → simpler language  
- Adult → deeper detail  

### Example:
- Tourist guide ≠ school report  
- Tabloid ≠ serious newspaper  

---

## 🧠 Key Question Before Writing

Ask:
> “Who is this for?”

This helps decide:
- Tone  
- Vocabulary  
- Detail  

👉 Writing improves instantly when a real reader is imagined  

---

## 🔚 Final Insight

Writing without a clear audience feels unfocused.

Writing with a clear audience:
- Feels purposeful  
- Feels natural  
- Scores higher
